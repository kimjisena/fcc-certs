Biography

Dennis MacAlistair Ritchie (born September 9, 1949) was an American computer scientist. Dennis Ritchie was born in Bronxville, New York to one Alistair E. Ritchie, a longtime Bell Labs scientist.

Dennis Ritchie graduated from Harvard University with degrees in Physics and applied mathematics. Ritchie began working at the Bell Labs Computing Sciences Research Center in 1967.

In the 1960s, Ritchie and his longtime colleague Ken Thompson worked on the Multics operating system at Bell Labs. Their experience on the Multics project greatly influenced the design and development of the Unix operating system later in 1969.

After a lifetime of remarkable contributions to the field of computer science and human life at large, Dennis Ritchie was found dead on October 12, 2011 at the age of 70 at his home in Berkeley Heights, New Jersey.

Contribution to human progress

"Unix and C are the ultimate computer viruses."
-- Richard Gabriel, Worse is Better, 1991

Dennis Ritchie is known for Unix and the C programming language.

The Unix operating system

The first version of the Unix operating system was developed by Ken Thompson. It was done entirely in assembly and hence was closely tied to the PDP-11 machine for which the system was first developed.

As Unix grew from simply a hobby system and Ken's playground to a serious operating system for office and scientific work, the question of portability became apparent. Dennis Ritchie played a major role in porting Unix specifically for his invention of the C programming language.

After the invention of the C programming language, the Unix operating system was re-written in C, first in part and later in full. This made it possible to run the system on different machines for which C compilers were available. In fact, C reduced the portability problem to a simple case of writing a compiler.

In addition to that, Ritchie did much of the Unix documentation work. He was known among his Bell Labs colleagues for his eloquent technical writing style and his dry wit.

"You're not suppossed to understand this."
-- Dennis Ritchie's comment in Unix source code.

The C programming language

Whenever the story of Unix is told, the story of the C programming language is also told. C was designed in the 1970s by Dennis Ritchie as an improvement on B, a systems programming language which was created by Ken Thompson. B itself was based off of BCPL.

The minimalist and terse design of C was closely tied to the design of the Unix operating system. Unlike other high-level languages, it is possible to master and use all of C's features in one lifetime.

The C programming language made possible the write-once-run-anywhere philosophy as it was used to port the Unix operating system. Despite being a small language, C has remained as the rock on which all modern technology infrastructure rests.

All major operating systems are written in C. C can be found in a wide range of devices from supercomputers to embedded devices. Additionally, the influence of C on major modern programming languages such as C++, Python, Java and JavaScript is self-evident.

Recognition

Dennis Ritchie has been recognised by individuals, national and international bodies alike for his Unix and C work.

  • 1983 - Turing Award (with Ken Thompson)
  • 1990 - IEEE Richard W. Hamming Medal (with Ken Thompson)
  • 1997 - Fellow of the Computer History Museum (with Ken Thompson)
  • 1999 - National Medal of Technology (with Ken Thompson)
  • 2005 - Industrial Research Institue Achievement Award
  • 2011 - Japan Prize for Information and Communications (with Ken Thompson)
  • 2012 - Asteroid 294727 Dennisritchie named in his memory
